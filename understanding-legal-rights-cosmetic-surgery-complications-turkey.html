Understanding Your Legal Rights After Cosmetic Surgery Complications in Turkey

Turkish law provides clear protections for patients when cosmetic procedures go wrong. This guide explains the legal framework, a landmark Court of Cassation ruling, and the practical steps you can take to pursue justice.

Introduction

For many people, traveling abroad for cosmetic surgery is a path toward feeling more comfortable in their own skin. Turkey has become a global hub for these procedures, welcoming thousands of international patients each year with world-class facilities and competitive pricing.

However, when an operation does not go as planned, the emotional and physical impact can be overwhelming. Discovering that a procedure has led to unexpected complications can leave you feeling lost — especially when navigating a foreign healthcare and legal system.

It is important to know that Turkish law provides strong framework protections for patient rights after plastic surgery in Turkey. Many patients facing these difficult circumstances seek guidance on how the law views medical negligence and unsatisfactory outcomes. This guide explains exactly that.

The Legal Context: Work Contracts and the "Guaranteed Result"

Under Turkish law, the legal relationship between a patient and a surgeon for an aesthetic procedure is uniquely categorised. According to consistent rulings by the Turkish Court of Cassation (Yargıtay), an agreement for cosmetic surgery is treated as a work contract (eser sözleşmesi) under the Turkish Code of Obligations — not as a standard service relationship.

This classification carries critical protections for patients facing aesthetic procedure problems in Turkey.

The Expectation of a Specific Result

Unlike general medical treatments where a doctor promises to use their best efforts to treat an illness, a cosmetic procedure is legally understood as an agreement to produce a specific, agreed-upon physical result. The surgeon is not merely promising to try — they are contractually committing to an outcome.

The Strict Duty of Care

Because the goal is a specific aesthetic outcome on a healthy individual, the surgeon is held to a strict duty of attention and care throughout the entire process — from pre-operative planning and the surgery itself to post-operative follow-up and record-keeping.

Complications vs. Fault: A Critical Distinction

While every surgery carries inherent medical risks, Turkish law draws a clear line between an unavoidable complication and an outcome caused by a surgeon's lack of foresight, inadequate planning, or failure to perform the procedure to professional standards. When a result falls significantly short of what was agreed upon — or causes physical deformities — patients have grounds to explore their rights regarding medical negligence in Turkey.

Real Patient Concerns: A Rhinoplasty Ruling by the Court of Cassation

To understand how these legal principles work in practice, we can examine the framework of a recent ruling by the Turkish Court of Cassation (6th Civil Chamber, Decision No. 2023/4310).

In this case, a patient underwent a rhinoplasty (nose surgery) to correct a structural shape issue. Following the initial operation, the patient noticed severe irregularities. The surgeon performed a second revision surgery to address them — but the structural problems worsened, resulting in physical indentations on the sides of the nose and deformities affecting the breathing tract.

Failure to Inform: Informed Consent Violations

Legal and medical experts reviewing the case identified that the physician had failed to properly warn and inform the patient about the specific potential risks and complications of the procedure. Under Turkish law, informed consent is not a formality — it is a substantive legal obligation. The patient must be given a full, clear explanation of risks in a language they understand, with adequate time to consider their decision.

Neglecting Medical Record-Keeping

The surgeon also failed to properly preserve the patient's medical examination records and surgical notes. This is an independent breach of professional duty. Courts treat missing or incomplete records as a serious failure — and may draw adverse inferences from the absence of documentation, strengthening the patient's position.

Violation of the Duty of Care

The physical outcome — deformities that worsened after a revision attempt — demonstrated that the surgeon failed to exercise the meticulous care required under a work contract. The promise to deliver a specific aesthetic result safely had not been kept.

The court ruled in the patient's favour, awarding:

  • A refund of the original surgery fees
  • Compensation for the cost of corrective surgery by a different surgeon
  • Moral damages for the deep emotional distress, pain, and suffering caused by the disfigurement

Financial and Emotional Recovery: What Can Be Claimed?

When international patients face cosmetic surgery complications, their primary goal is usually finding a qualified specialist to correct the outcome. Understanding what a legal remedy covers helps you plan your next steps with clarity. Under Turkish law, compensation is divided into two categories.

1. Material Damages (Maddi Tazminat)

Material damages address the direct financial losses caused by the faulty procedure. They can include:

  • A full or partial refund of the fees paid for the unsuccessful surgery
  • The estimated cost of the necessary revision or corrective surgery
  • Medical documentation and expert reports establishing the financial burden of corrective treatment

Courts require clear evidence to quantify these amounts — making your independent medical assessment and financial records critically important.

2. Moral Damages (Manevi Tazminat)

Aesthetic complications on visible areas — the face, nose, breasts, or body — often cause deep psychological suffering, social anxiety, and a lasting loss of self-confidence. Turkish law allows for spiritual compensation to alleviate this emotional trauma.

Courts determine the amount based on the severity of the physical damage, the degree of the surgeon's fault, and the circumstances of the parties. Notably, higher courts — including the Court of Cassation — regularly overturn low compensation awards, emphasising that moral damages must be sufficient to provide genuine emotional relief and justice, not merely a token recognition of suffering.

Navigating Your Next Steps

If you are currently dealing with a botched or unsatisfactory cosmetic result from a clinic in Turkey, it is completely normal to feel uncertain about dealing with a foreign legal process. The following practical steps can protect your position and preserve your options.

Request Your Complete Medical Records

Obtain a full copy of your medical file from the Turkish clinic. This should include pre-operative photos, surgical notes, anesthesia records, and discharge reports. Request these documents as early as possible — ideally before leaving the clinic or as soon as a dispute arises. If the clinic refuses, a legal representative can compel disclosure through the Ministry of Health or the courts.

Document Everything

Take clear, dated photographs of the complications as they develop. Keep all receipts for surgery fees, travel, accommodation, and any medical treatment you have sought since. Save every message, email, and call log from communications with the clinic — these form a crucial timeline of events.

Seek an Independent Medical Evaluation

Consult an independent plastic surgeon or medical board in your home country to obtain an objective assessment. Their report should address whether your current condition is a normal healing phase or a permanent complication requiring revision. This independent evaluation is essential: courts rely on objective medical expert findings to establish fault and quantify the harm you have suffered.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is cosmetic surgery legally classified in Turkey?

Turkish courts classify cosmetic surgery agreements as a work contract (eser sözleşmesi) under the Turkish Code of Obligations. This means the surgeon is legally obligated to deliver a specific agreed physical result — not simply to use their best efforts as in general medicine. The distinction gives patients significantly stronger legal protections when outcomes fall short of what was promised.

What is the difference between a complication and negligence under Turkish law?

A complication is a known medical risk that can occur even when a surgeon performs to professional standards — and for which the surgeon cannot be held liable, provided they properly informed the patient beforehand. Negligence occurs when an outcome is caused by the surgeon's lack of foresight, inadequate planning, failure to inform the patient of specific risks, or failure to perform to the accepted professional standard. The distinction is what determines your right to compensation.

What did the Court of Cassation rule in Decision No. 2023/4310?

In Decision No. 2023/4310, the 6th Civil Chamber found a rhinoplasty surgeon liable for three failures: inadequate informed consent, failure to preserve surgical records, and delivery of a result that caused physical deformities and breathing difficulties. The patient was awarded a refund of surgery fees, the cost of future corrective surgery by another surgeon, and moral damages for emotional suffering.

What compensation can I claim if my cosmetic surgery went wrong in Turkey?

You can claim material damages (maddi tazminat) covering the refund of surgery fees and the cost of corrective procedures, and moral damages (manevi tazminat) for psychological suffering, social anxiety, and loss of self-confidence. Turkish higher courts regularly increase moral damage awards when lower courts set amounts that do not genuinely reflect the severity of the patient's suffering.

The surgeon failed to keep my medical records. Does that affect my case?

Yes, significantly. The duty to preserve accurate medical and surgical records is a professional obligation under Turkish law. A surgeon's failure to maintain proper documentation is treated as an independent breach of duty. Courts may draw adverse inferences from missing records, which can meaningfully strengthen your compensation claim.

Do I need to return to Turkey to pursue a legal claim?

No. International patients can pursue legal claims in Turkey entirely from abroad by granting a power of attorney to a Turkish lawyer — through your local Turkish Consulate or via a notarised document bearing an Apostille. Your attorney can handle all court filings, expert correspondence, and hearings on your behalf.

Knowing Your Options

Every medical situation is unique, and the legal path depends heavily on the specific details of your contract, the consent forms you signed, and the medical reports from your procedure. Turkish law provides clear avenues to hold medical providers accountable and support patients in navigating these issues.
